# Knicks-Spurs Game 3 Reaction: The Loss Was Close, But the Regression Was Real

The Knicks finally lost.

After more than 40 days without a playoff loss, after 13 straight postseason wins, after stealing two games in San Antonio and bringing the NBA Finals back to Madison Square Garden for the first time since 1999, the Knicks dropped Game 3 to the Spurs, 115-111.

And the immediate reaction should not be panic. This is still a series the Knicks lead 2-1. This is still a Knicks team that has not looked outclassed at any point in these Finals. Even in defeat, this was not a game where the Spurs exposed New York as a fraud. The Knicks lost by four. They have now lost three total games in this playoff run, and none of them have been the kind of loss that makes you question whether they belong. Every loss has basically come down to a possession, a whistle, a bad stretch, or a late-game failure to clean up one more detail.

But that is also why this one is frustrating.

Because the Knicks were close enough to win, talented enough to win, deep enough to win, and still somehow looked like they were moving backward from the team they were earlier in the playoffs.

This was not a disaster. It was worse in a different way. It was a warning.

## The game was right there, but the Knicks lost the shape of it

The final score says 115-111. The quarter breakdown tells the story more honestly.

San Antonio came out sharp and won the first quarter 33-22. Then the Knicks exploded for 42 points in the second quarter, turning what looked like an early Spurs road statement into a halftime lead for New York. That second quarter was the version of the Knicks that can win the championship: fast decisions, ball movement, OG Anunoby punishing gaps, Josh Hart turning chaos into momentum, Brunson still controlling the emotional rhythm of the game.

Then the second half happened.

The Knicks scored 47 points after halftime. They had 27 in the third and only 20 in the fourth. For a team that has built this playoff run around late-game composure, toughness, and the idea that they eventually solve whatever defense is thrown at them, this was one of the first times they looked stuck.

The Spurs did not blow them out. They did not dominate every minute. They did not suddenly become unbeatable. But they changed the pressure of the game, and the Knicks did not answer it the way they answered Atlanta, Cleveland, or earlier versions of San Antonio.

That is the difference.

Against the Hawks, the Knicks eventually figured out how to turn pressure into punishment. Atlanta could speed the game up, bother Brunson, throw bodies at the ball, and make the first few games annoying. But once the Knicks adjusted, they blew the series open. They closed that first-round series with a 140-89 Game 6, one of those games where a team stops negotiating with the opponent and simply removes them from the court.

Against the Spurs, the Knicks have not found that same release valve yet. They have the potential to blow San Antonio out. You can feel it. There are stretches where the Spurs look young, rushed, turnover-prone, and unsure of how to handle the Knicks' physicality. But there are also long stretches where San Antonio's length and defensive pressure make New York shrink the floor.

That is the tension of this series.

The Knicks are good enough to end games early. The Spurs are good enough to never let them breathe.

## What the postgame answers said

The Spurs' postgame answers made the loss feel less random. NBA.com's takeaways noted that Wembanyama pointed to better communication: knowing coverages, knowing offensive and defensive calls, and getting teammates organized earlier in possessions. That is exactly what the Knicks felt after halftime. The floor did not just get crowded because San Antonio is long. It got crowded because San Antonio was earlier, louder, and more connected.

Mike Brown's answer was the Knicks side of the same problem. He described the offense as stagnant and said too many possessions turned into one player dribbling while everyone else watched. That is why this is an opinion piece instead of a plain game summary. The box score says four-point loss. The postgame answers said the Spurs had found a pressure point.

## The whistle in the second half changed the feel of the game

There is no way to talk about Game 3 honestly without talking about the whistle.

This is not to say the Knicks lost only because of officiating. They did enough wrong on their own. They turned the ball over 13 times. They missed too many open looks. They let Victor Wembanyama get comfortable early and then let him regain control late. They did not get enough from Mikal Bridges. Karl-Anthony Towns was too quiet. Landry Shamet was given chances and did not make enough of them.

But in the second half, the foul discrepancy felt like part of the game, not a side note.

The Spurs were allowed to play with a certain level of pressure, contact, grabbing, bumping, and bodying that the Knicks did not seem to be allowed to match possession for possession. When the game is this close, that matters. A few free throws are not just a few free throws. They change how aggressive a defender can be. They change how Towns contests. They change how Brunson attacks. They change whether a team can play through contact or starts expecting the contact to go unrewarded.

The full-game numbers support why the Knicks should be annoyed: San Antonio shot 32 free throws, while New York shot 22. The Spurs made 25 of them. The Knicks made 18. That is a seven-point difference at the line in a four-point game.

Again, that does not erase the Knicks' mistakes. It does not erase the fact that the Spurs earned some of those trips by attacking downhill and putting pressure on the rim. But it does explain why the second half felt different. The Knicks were not just playing the Spurs' defense. They were playing a version of the game where San Antonio's physicality seemed to compound, and New York's responses seemed to come with a higher cost.

The Knicks cannot let that become their excuse. But they also cannot pretend it had nothing to do with the rhythm of the game.

## Karl-Anthony Towns has to be more than a pressure release valve

Karl-Anthony Towns finished with 11 points, eight rebounds, and only 10 field goal attempts.

That cannot happen in this series.

Towns does not need to score 30 every night for the Knicks to win. That is not the point. The point is involvement. When the Spurs pressure Brunson, when they crowd the ball, when they use Wembanyama's length to make every drive feel like it is happening inside a forest, Towns has to become the player who breaks the defense open.

If he is only a spot-up option, the Knicks are helping San Antonio.

Towns is too skilled, too big, and too important to float in and out of the game. He has to touch the ball where he can force the Spurs to make an uncomfortable decision. If Wembanyama is near the rim, make him defend in space. If San Antonio switches smaller players onto Towns, punish it before the double comes. If the Spurs load up on Brunson, run more offense through KAT at the elbow or in early drag actions before the defense is set.

This is where New York has to be careful. In the regular season, and even through much of the playoffs, the Knicks have had the luxury of letting Brunson solve games through force of will. But San Antonio is not giving them the same type of problem. Wembanyama changes the geometry. He makes normal drives abnormal. He makes floaters feel rushed. He makes passing windows disappear.

That means Towns cannot just be involved when the game naturally finds him. The Knicks have to intentionally involve him.

In Game 3, he looked less like a co-star and more like a player waiting for the game to come back to him.

That is not enough.

## Mikal Bridges cannot disappear in a Finals game

Mikal Bridges had two points.

There is no softer way to say that. Two points in 29 minutes. One made field goal. Five shot attempts. Most of the fourth quarter on the bench.

For a player this important to the Knicks' roster construction, that is not just a bad box score. That is a structural problem.

Bridges is not on this team to be a passive fifth option. He is on this team because he gives the Knicks the outline of a complete playoff roster: length, defense, secondary creation, transition finishing, and enough scoring to punish teams for overloading on Brunson and Towns. When Bridges is aggressive, the Knicks look like the kind of team that has no weak link. When Bridges disappears, the Spurs can tilt harder toward Brunson and dare the rest of the floor to create.

And that is exactly what happened.

This is where the Knicks have to be honest with themselves. Bridges does not have to become a 25-point scorer overnight. But he has to be present. He has to cut harder. He has to attack closeouts. He has to look at mismatches as opportunities instead of interruptions. He has to be part of the pressure on San Antonio, not just a spacer waiting for the ball to arrive with five seconds left.

The Knicks have enough players who can contribute. But in the Finals, contribution is not enough. Impact matters.

Bridges has to impact Game 4.

## Landry Shamet got the shots, but not the payoff

Landry Shamet has been one of the reasons this Knicks run has felt deeper than past Knicks teams. He gives them movement shooting, spacing, and the kind of bench scoring that changes a playoff rotation. But Game 3 was one of those nights where the role was there, the shots were there, and the payoff was not.

Shamet finished with three points on 1-of-8 shooting, including 1-of-7 from three.

That is brutal in a game decided by four.

But this is not just about blaming Shamet for missing shots. Shooters miss. That is the job. The bigger issue is how the Knicks' offense looked when those shots did not fall. If Shamet is not hitting, New York still has to make his minutes functional. They cannot let his misses become dead possessions that feed Spurs transition or allow San Antonio to stay attached to Brunson without paying a price.

This is where the bench difference showed up. San Antonio got 13 points and nine rebounds from Dylan Harper. They got seven points from Keldon Johnson in only 17 minutes. They got just enough from their support pieces to bridge the gap between Wembanyama's superstar stretches.

The Knicks' bench had moments, but it did not have enough weight. Jordan Clarkson gave them 10. Jose Alvarado gave them four. Mitchell Robinson gave them five. But the lineup combinations did not tilt the game the way they had in previous rounds.

The Knicks' bench has been one of their advantages all postseason. In Game 3, it was closer to neutral, and when the starters were not all firing, neutral was not enough.

## Wembanyama finally controlled the terms

Victor Wembanyama had 32 points, eight rebounds, six assists, and three blocks.

But the numbers are not even the full story. The real problem was that he looked comfortable early and dangerous late.

That is the version of Wembanyama the Knicks cannot allow to settle into this series. In Games 1 and 2, New York made him work through frustration. They forced him into mistakes. They made him look like a young player still learning how to process Finals pressure. In Game 3, he looked like the player San Antonio believes can break every normal basketball rule.

He scored nine points in the first five minutes. That matters. It let the Spurs breathe. It let them believe. It let the youngest star in the series feel like Madison Square Garden was not too loud, not too big, and not too fast.

From there, the Knicks were reacting.

And then Stephon Castle gave San Antonio the second punch they needed. Castle finished with 23 points, five rebounds, and five assists. He was not just surviving the moment. He was adding to it.

That is the real concern. If Wembanyama is great, the Knicks can still win. They have already shown that. But if Wembanyama is great and San Antonio's supporting guards start hitting big shots, attacking downhill, and making the Knicks defend multiple actions in a row, the series gets uncomfortable fast.

## This is not panic, but Game 4 is a test of problem-solving

The Knicks do not need to reinvent themselves before Game 4.

They need to remember why they got here.

They got here because they are not a one-man team. They got here because Brunson could be great without the offense becoming small. They got here because Towns changed matchups. Because Bridges gave them two-way shape. Because OG Anunoby could swing a game without needing permission. Because Josh Hart could turn rebounds into energy. Because the bench could stretch leads instead of simply surviving minutes.

Game 3 made the Knicks look smaller than that.

The score was close, but the offense felt compressed. The game was winnable, but the decision-making felt rushed. The Spurs were beatable, but the Knicks played too much of the second half like they were trying to solve the pressure one possession at a time instead of bending the entire defense.

That is the adjustment.

More Towns involvement. More Bridges aggression. Cleaner Shamet usage. Fewer dead possessions. Better emotional control when the whistle feels uneven. More pressure on San Antonio before Wembanyama gets to sit in the paint and turn the game into a math problem the Knicks do not want to take.

The Knicks can blow this Spurs team out. That is still on the table. We saw the version of the Knicks that can do it in the second quarter. We saw it against Atlanta. We have seen it throughout this entire playoff run.

But the Finals do not reward potential. They reward adjustment.

And Game 3 was the first real sign that the Knicks are no longer just chasing the championship.

They are being chased back.

Game 4 will tell us whether this was just a bad night after 40-plus days of winning, or whether the Spurs have found the pressure point the Knicks still have to solve.
