# Knicks-Spurs Game 3 Postgame: The First Real Adjustment Problem

## Episode Summary

The Knicks did not get blown out in Game 3. That is the first thing to say. The final was Spurs 115, Knicks 111, and the game was close enough that a few cleaner possessions, a few better counters, or a more balanced whistle could have changed the entire mood of the night.

But the loss still mattered because it was the first time in these Finals that San Antonio made New York look smaller. The Spurs changed the pressure of the game after halftime, Victor Wembanyama found comfort early and control late, and the Knicks never quite solved the way San Antonio was shrinking the floor around Jalen Brunson.

This episode is about the adjustment problem. It is about Karl-Anthony Towns needing more intentional touches, Mikal Bridges needing to be more than a spacer, Landry Shamet missing the shots that usually loosen the defense, and Tom Thibodeau needing to make Game 4 feel less like a possession-by-possession stress test.

## Key Topics

- Why a four-point loss can still reveal a structural problem
- The second-half whistle and how foul pressure changed New York's aggression
- Victor Wembanyama's early rhythm and late control
- Karl-Anthony Towns as the pressure release valve San Antonio has to respect
- Mikal Bridges' quiet night and why the Knicks need more downhill force
- Game 4 adjustments that can reopen the floor for Brunson

## Show Notes

The first segment resets the series context: New York still leads 2-1, but San Antonio now has a template it can bring into Game 4. The conversation starts with the second quarter, when the Knicks looked like a team ready to crack the game open, then moves into the second half, when the offense got stuck and the Spurs kept forcing late-clock decisions.

The middle of the episode focuses on usage. Towns finished with only 10 field goal attempts, Bridges scored two points, and Shamet shot 1-of-8. The point is not to turn the postgame into a blame list. The point is that the Knicks cannot beat San Antonio's length if too many possessions end with Brunson trying to solve the entire floor by himself.

The closing segment is about Game 4. The Knicks do not need panic changes. They need sharper spacing, earlier Towns touches, more aggressive Bridges decisions, and enough emotional control to keep the whistle from becoming the story of the offense.

## Transcript

[00:00] Welcome back to Concrete Cast. The Knicks finally lost a playoff game, and the strange part is that it was not a disaster. It was not the kind of loss where you walk away thinking the better team has announced itself. It was a four-point Finals game. The Knicks are still up 2-1. But it was also the first time in weeks where they looked like they were reacting instead of solving.

[03:40] The second quarter is why this one is frustrating. New York scored 42 in the quarter. The ball moved. OG Anunoby hit gaps. Josh Hart turned loose possessions into energy. Brunson was still controlling the emotional temperature. That version of the Knicks can win the title. The problem is that it only showed up in a full, connected way for one quarter.

[09:15] The Spurs changed the feel after halftime. Some of that was the whistle. San Antonio shot 32 free throws, New York shot 22, and in a four-point game that matters. But the Knicks also have to own the possessions where the ball stuck, the spacing tightened, and the offense turned into Brunson trying to create through two bodies with Wembanyama waiting behind the play.

[15:50] Towns has to be the release valve. Not just a spot-up big. Not just a player who gets the ball when the possession happens to find him. If San Antonio is loading up on Brunson, the Knicks have to make Towns an active problem. Touches at the elbow, quick slips, early drag actions, anything that forces Wembanyama to defend a decision instead of just defending the rim.

[21:30] Bridges is the other piece. Two points in a Finals game is not enough, but the number matters less than the presence. The Knicks need him cutting, attacking closeouts, and making San Antonio pay for tilting toward Brunson and Towns. If Bridges is passive, the Spurs can make the floor feel crowded.

[27:10] Game 4 is not about panic. It is about whether the Knicks can adjust before the series becomes a new problem. They have shown the version of themselves that can blow this matchup open. They just need to make that version last longer than one quarter.
