# Why Tom Thibodeau Could Build the Knicks, But Mike Brown Could Finish Them

There is a difference between building a team and completing one.

Tom Thibodeau built the Knicks back into something real. That part should not be erased, no matter how this ended. He gave the franchise structure, defense, accountability, and a serious basketball identity after years where the Knicks barely felt like an NBA organization. He helped turn Jalen Brunson into the face of the city. He gave Madison Square Garden playoff basketball again. He took the Knicks to the Eastern Conference Finals for the first time in 25 years.

But that is also why the ending is so complicated.

Because the question was never whether Thibodeau could make the Knicks respectable. He already did that. The question was whether he could take a team with championship-level talent and meet the moment with championship-level flexibility.

That is where he failed.

And that is where Mike Brown succeeded.

![Tom Thibodeau and Mike Brown representing two different Knicks coaching eras](/images/blog/knicks-thibs-brown-hero.jpg)
*Thibodeau restored the Knicks' foundation. Brown gave the roster room to evolve.*

## Thibs gave the Knicks a floor. Brown gave them a ceiling.

The easiest version of this argument is to say Thibodeau was bad and Brown was good. That is not true. Thibs was necessary. He was the adult in the room when the Knicks needed one. He made the Knicks hard to play against again. He made losing uncomfortable again.

But the same things that made him valuable at the beginning became the same things that limited him at the end.

His trust was hard to earn and easy to lose. His rotations were rigid. His lineups were often treated like stone tablets. His favorites played through exhaustion, while players outside the circle had to be perfect just to get a real chance. That works when you are trying to create a culture. It breaks down when the culture is already built and the playoffs become about adjustment.

That was the difference with Brown. He inherited the structure Thibodeau created, but he did not treat the structure like a prison. He was willing to use the full roster. He was willing to have uncomfortable conversations. He was willing to adapt before the series was already slipping away.

That is why this title feels like a continuation and a correction at the same time.

## The player relationships always felt fragile under Thibs

The first warning sign with Thibodeau was not one single player. It was the pattern.

Young players and fringe rotation players often felt like they were living on the edge of exile. RJ Barrett never fully became the player the Knicks hoped he would become in New York before being moved in the OG Anunoby trade. Obi Toppin, the No. 8 pick, never got the kind of runway Knicks fans begged to see. Austin Rivers later spoke openly about how his Knicks experience under Thibodeau did not go well. Landry Shamet, who became a real piece in this championship run, is another example of how different a player can look when the coach actually gives him a role instead of making him live possession to possession.

That does not mean every player was secretly miserable. It also does not mean every player who left would have become a star if Thibs had just played him more. Some of this is roster construction. Some of this is timing. Some of this is the hard reality of the NBA.

But the common thread was obvious: Thibodeau coached like trust was something players had to survive long enough to receive.

Brown coached like trust was something he could give early, then demand players justify later.

That sounds small until the playoffs arrive and your season depends on the eighth man, the ninth man, or the guy who has not played real minutes in two weeks walking into a fire and acting like he belongs there.

Under Thibodeau, those players often looked like emergency options.

Under Brown, they looked like part of the plan.

## The bench was not just depth. It was belief.

This is where Tyler Kolek and Landry Shamet matter.

Kolek was not just some cute regular-season story. He gave the Knicks real minutes in the NBA Cup run, including the Spurs matchup, where New York showed early in the season that its bench could change games. Those minutes mattered because they built evidence. They told the locker room that the Knicks were not five players and a prayer. They were a team.

Shamet is the bigger symbol.

There are certain role players who only become useful when the coach lets them be useful. Shamet is not supposed to be the face of a playoff run. He is not supposed to be the guy you build around. But he is absolutely the kind of player who swings a series when he is trusted, spaced correctly, and allowed to shoot without looking over his shoulder after one mistake.

That is what Brown gave him.

When Shamet hit big shots, it was not random. It was the product of a coach creating a role before the moment came. That is the difference between hoping a bench player saves you and preparing a bench player to save you.

Thibodeau too often lived in the first version.

Brown lived in the second.

## The foul-up-three problem was the whole Thibodeau era in one decision

For me, the clearest sign of Thibodeau's stubbornness goes all the way back before he was even the Knicks coach.

April 8, 2012. Easter Sunday. Bulls vs. Knicks at the Garden. Carmelo Anthony walks into a three over Luol Deng and sends the game to overtime. Then he hits another three in overtime to win it.

That game is remembered as a Melo classic, and it should be. But it is also one of the first moments where you can see the old-school basketball brain refusing to bend. The Bulls had the chance to take away the three. They did not. Melo punished them.

The detail that matters is not just that Carmelo hit the shot. It is that the broadcast almost froze the decision in real time. Mike Breen raised the question every Knicks fan would remember years later: should Chicago foul up three? Jeff Van Gundy, a product of the same old-school defensive basketball universe as Thibodeau, represented the old instinct not to foul. And that answer became the philosophy. Trust the defense. Do not give the foul. Do not bend to the situation. Make them hit the shot.

Melo hit the shot.

That should have been the lesson.

![Carmelo Anthony shooting over Luol Deng during the Knicks Bulls Easter 2012 game](/images/blog/carmelo-bulls-easter-2012.jpg)
*Carmelo Anthony's Easter 2012 three over Luol Deng became an early warning about refusing to foul up three.*

Instead, more than a decade later, the same idea came back in a Knicks uniform.

April 8, 2025. Knicks up three against the Celtics. Jayson Tatum gets a chance at a three with 2.9 seconds left. He makes it. The game goes to overtime. Boston wins.

This was the basketball gods giving Thibodeau a warning before the real exam. It was not a playoff loss. It was not the series. It was the mistake before the mistake. Tatum's three with 2.9 seconds left forced overtime, Boston won, and the Knicks got a free chance to learn something before the games became permanent.

![Jayson Tatum hitting a late three against the Knicks to force overtime](/images/blog/tatum-knicks-overtime-three-2025.jpg)
*Jayson Tatum's tying three with 2.9 seconds left was the regular-season warning before the playoff collapse.*

Then the playoffs arrived, and the lesson came back with teeth.

Game 1 against Indiana was not just a blown lead. It was the full price of refusing to evolve. The Knicks were up 14 with under three minutes left. Aaron Nesmith hit three after three. Tyrese Haliburton survived the final possession, forced overtime, and Indiana stole a game the Knicks had no business losing.

The only way to lose was to let the Pacers keep playing the math game. The only way to lose was to allow threes, preserve clock, and let momentum become oxygen.

Thibodeau let it happen.

That is why the moment mattered. It was not one missed adjustment. It was the third version of the same mistake. Carmelo in 2012. Tatum in 2025. Nesmith and Haliburton in the Eastern Conference Finals. At some point, stubbornness stops being principle and starts becoming negligence.

![Aaron Nesmith and Tyrese Haliburton during the Pacers Game 1 comeback against the Knicks](/images/blog/pacers-knicks-game1-nesmith-haliburton.jpg)
*Aaron Nesmith's late fourth-quarter eruption and Tyrese Haliburton's survival act made Game 1 the price of not learning.*

There is nuance to fouling up three. It is not automatic in every situation. Coaches have to consider time, personnel, free-throw shooters, whether the foul can happen before the shooting motion, and whether the defense is organized.

But the point is not that Thibodeau had to foul every single time.

The point is that he seemed philosophically opposed to the idea even when the game was screaming for it.

That is what doomed him. Not one decision. The refusal to evolve from the decision.

## Brown won because he did not fight the roster

Mike Brown did not win because he came in and erased everything Thibodeau built. He won because he kept the good parts and removed the ceiling.

That is the difference.

Thibodeau built the habits. Brown widened the imagination.

Brown involved new players. He listened to his assistants. He was malleable in a way Thibodeau never really was. He was not afraid to look at the game in front of him and admit that the original plan was not enough. If the Knicks needed a different lineup, he tried it. If they needed a different rhythm, he searched for it. If the bench had to rescue a stretch, he let the bench breathe long enough to do it.

That mattered most in Game 4 against the Spurs.

When the Knicks were down and the game looked like it was slipping away, Brown did not shrink the roster. He expanded it. He searched deeper into the bench and through different combinations. He treated the bench not like a last resort, but like a living part of the team. That is how trust becomes real. Not in a press conference. Not in a quote. In the middle of the Finals, when the season is shaking, and the coach is still willing to try something new.

That was the difference between the two eras.

Under Thibodeau, the Knicks often felt like they had to win through the same answers, even when the question had changed.

Under Brown, the Knicks were allowed to change the answer.

And then came the closeout game.

Game 5 in San Antonio, Knicks up three, championship on the line. This was the ghost that had followed the franchise from Easter 2012 to Tatum in 2025 to the Pacers collapse. The moment was not theoretical anymore. It was not a regular-season lesson. It was not a conference finals warning. It was the NBA Finals, on the road, with a championship sitting one possession away.

This time, the Knicks fouled.

With 8.5 seconds left and New York up 93-90, Landry Shamet took the foul before San Antonio could launch the tying three. Dylan Harper went to the line. The Spurs did not get to play the old game.

That one decision encapsulated everything.

Thibodeau's Knicks trusted the old rule and got burned by it. Brown's Knicks remembered the scar tissue and acted on it. They did not let San Antonio take the one shot that could change history. They did not let the game become a coin flip. They did not let stubbornness dress itself up as toughness.

They made the correct basketball play.

That is what growth looks like.

## Thibodeau was the foundation. Brown was the evolution.

The cruel part is that Thibodeau was not a failed Knicks coach in the normal sense. He is one of the most important coaches the franchise has had in decades. He helped turn a joke into a contender. He made the Knicks matter again.

But sometimes the coach who builds the house is not the coach who can finish it.

The Knicks outgrew Thibodeau because they needed flexibility more than control. They needed trust beyond the top six. They needed a coach who could listen, adjust, experiment, and learn from the exact mistakes that had buried them before.

Mike Brown did not succeed because he was the opposite of Thibodeau.

He succeeded because he was willing to evolve where Thibodeau would not.

Carmelo was the warning. Tatum was the reminder. Indiana was the punishment.

San Antonio was the correction.

That is why this championship does not erase Thibs.

It explains him.

![Mike Brown coaching the Knicks during the 2026 NBA Finals](/images/blog/mike-brown-knicks-finals-game5.jpg)
*In the closeout game, Brown's Knicks made the adjustment Thibodeau's teams never did: they protected the lead by taking away the tying three.*

He was the coach who made the Knicks serious enough to dream again.

Mike Brown was the coach who finally let the dream breathe.
