# Why Did It Take the Knicks 27 Years to Get Back?

As I am writing this on June 8, 2026, the Knicks are back in the NBA Finals for the first time since 1999 and lead the San Antonio Spurs 2-0 with Game 3 set for Madison Square Garden tonight. That means for an entire generation of Knicks fans, the Finals were not even a real memory. They were a VHS tape. A Patrick Ewing clip. A Jeff Van Gundy hanging onto Alonzo Mourning’s leg clip. A thing your dad or uncle talked about like it happened in another country.

So the obvious question becomes: how does a team in New York, with Madison Square Garden, with the money, the attention, the fanbase, and the pressure, go 27 years without getting back to the promised land?

The easy answer is to say the Knicks were cursed, or badly owned, or always dysfunctional. Some of that is true. But the deeper answer is more boring and more important: the Knicks spent most of those years living in the NBA’s worst possible place.

They were not good enough to win a championship.

They were not bad enough to reset properly.

And they were too impatient to admit either one.

That is the middle of the NBA. That is purgatory. That is where teams convince themselves that a first-round exit means they are “close,” when really they are still missing the pieces that playoff basketball exposes immediately.

## Thirty teams means nobody is entitled to a Finals run

Before even getting into Knicks history, it is worth admitting something simple: there are 30 teams in the NBA. In a perfectly even world, a team getting to the Finals every 15 years and winning a title every 30 years would actually be pretty normal.

So purely from the math, the Knicks waiting 27 years to get back is not completely insane.

But sports do not work like clean math. Markets matter. Ownership matters. Stars matter. Front offices matter. And the Knicks are not the Hornets or the Magic or the Grizzlies. They are the Knicks. The standard is different because the advantages are different.

The problem is that the Knicks kept acting like being the Knicks was a roster-building strategy.

It is not.

You do not win because the building is famous. You do not win because celebrities sit courtside. You do not win because free agents should want New York. You win because the roster makes sense, because the timing makes sense, because the contracts make sense, and because the five guys on the floor can survive the postseason when every weakness gets hunted.

That last part is what the Knicks missed for most of the 2000s and 2010s.

## The Knicks never really tanked, and that matters

One of the biggest differences between the Knicks and a franchise like the San Antonio Spurs is that the Spurs were willing, or at least able, to fall into the bottom at exactly the right moment.

The Spurs had won 59 games in 1995-96. Then David Robinson got hurt, the season collapsed, San Antonio finished 20-62, won the 1997 lottery, drafted Tim Duncan, and suddenly a great organization had a generational player next to Robinson. That one disaster year became a dynasty.

That is not luck alone. It is luck meeting a clean organizational situation.

The Knicks, by contrast, almost never committed to being terrible in the way that gives you a real shot at the best player in the draft. Even when they were bad, they were often bad in an accidental, scrambled way. Bad because the roster failed. Bad because the plan collapsed. Bad because the contracts were ugly. Not bad because they were patiently collecting assets and building toward a coherent future.

That distinction matters.

Tanking by itself does not make you smart. There are teams that tank forever and go nowhere. But refusing to bottom out while also not having a championship core is how you end up drafting in the wrong range, signing the wrong veterans, and convincing yourself the next quick fix will solve the last quick fix.

The Knicks were not rebuilding.

They were repairing.

And repairing is not the same thing as building.

## The 1990s Knicks were great, but greatness can leave a bill

The 1990s Knicks are not the same category as the disaster years. Those teams were legitimate. They had Patrick Ewing, real defense, real playoff identity, real hatred with the Heat and Pacers, and real chances.

They made the Finals in 1994 and 1999. They were tough enough to matter. They were complete enough to compete. But they were also running into Michael Jordan’s Bulls, Hakeem Olajuwon’s Rockets, Reggie Miller’s Pacers, and then Tim Duncan’s Spurs.

That era was not a failure in the normal sense. It was a good team in a brutal league.

But here is the hidden cost: being good for that long meant the Knicks were never drafting at the top. The 1990s Knicks were built to win now, which is fine, but when that window closed, there was no young superstar waiting underneath it. There was no clean succession plan.

So when the Ewing era ended, the Knicks tried to keep squeezing relevance out of the remaining pieces. Allan Houston. Latrell Sprewell. Then eventually Stephon Marbury. Then more names. More money. More “maybe this works.”

The franchise did not gracefully transition from one era to the next. It dragged the old era behind it like furniture through a narrow hallway.

By the time the Knicks finally admitted the old team was gone, they had already spent years making moves that kept them interesting enough to sell hope but not strong enough to matter.

## The Carmelo era had a star, but not the structure

Carmelo Anthony was not the problem in New York. The problem was that the Knicks treated having Carmelo like it solved more than it actually solved.

Melo gave the Knicks a real scorer, a real star, a real reason to watch. In 2012-13, that team won 54 games, won the Atlantic Division, and finally won a playoff series. That was not nothing. For a franchise that had spent years being a punchline, that season mattered.

But even that team was not built like a long-term championship machine. It was older, thinner, and dependent on a very specific balance. Raymond Felton and Jason Kidd gave them enough point guard structure for a season, Tyson Chandler gave them defense, J.R. Smith gave them scoring, and Melo gave them the offensive engine.

But once the margins slipped, the whole thing slipped.

The Linsanity moment in 2012 showed something the Knicks had been missing: pace, pressure, a real table-setter, somebody who could bend the defense before Melo even got the ball. Jeremy Lin was not the final answer to everything, but the reaction to him proved the point. Knicks fans were starving for guard play because the roster had been built around scoring without enough creation.

And this is important because that era happened before the league became what it is now. Today, there are more 20-point scoring guards than teams can even handle. Back then, a real point guard was not as easy to find. The Lakers were still trying to squeeze title basketball out of Derek Fisher types. The league was changing, but the Knicks were late to the change.

So the Knicks had Carmelo, but they did not have enough structure around him. They had a star, but not the complete machine. That is how you win 54 games one year and fall right back into confusion the next.

## The middle is where teams lie to themselves

The NBA is full of teams that hit on a great player and then immediately become too good to keep building the clean way.

That sounds like a good problem, but it can become a trap.

Look at Dallas with Luka. The Mavericks were good because Luka was good enough to drag them into relevance almost immediately. But that also made it harder to keep adding elite young talent around him. They eventually made the Finals in 2024, but they ran into a Boston team that was deeper, cleaner, and more complete. Luka was good enough to get them there. The roster was not complete enough to finish the job.

That is not an insult. That is the NBA.

A superstar can cover problems from November to April. In May and June, the problems come back with names, matchups, and scouting reports.

The Lakers have lived in a version of this too. They cashed in the future to maximize LeBron and Anthony Davis. They got a title out of it, so you cannot call it a failure. But once you sell tomorrow for today, today better be complete. If the present roster becomes incomplete and the future is already gone, there is no easy button left.

The Pacers are another version of the same question. They went from being in the Finals to Haliburton’s Achilles changing the direction of the franchise. Pascal Siakam is still good, but he is aging. The team had a bad season, hoped the lottery would rescue them, and then the lottery did what the lottery does: it introduced randomness. If you tank and still miss the pick, now you have the pain without the prize.

That is why the middle is so dangerous.

The middle tells you: “We are close.”

Sometimes you are.

A lot of the time, you are just expensive.

## Boston is the warning sign hiding in plain sight

The Celtics are the example people do not want to hear because they won. But that is exactly why they are interesting.

Boston had a real championship team. They had Tatum, Brown, Holiday, Porzingis, White, Horford, shooting, defense, size, and continuity. That is a complete roster. That is why they won.

But the second-apron NBA is not kind to expensive teams. Once Tatum and Brown are both on monster contracts, the question becomes: can the roster around them stay complete? Can you keep enough shooting, defense, ballhandling, and size around two huge salaries? Can you survive an injury? Can you survive losing the veterans that made the stars’ lives easier?

This is where teams get sentimental. They say, “Our core won, so our core is enough.”

But the core is not always enough. The core wins when the ecosystem around it is right.

The 2024 Celtics were not just Tatum and Brown. They were Tatum and Brown with Jrue Holiday, Derrick White, Porzingis, Horford, spacing, defense, and almost no obvious weak spot. Once that structure gets more expensive and less complete, the same stars can become a less dangerous team.

That is why I do not buy the idea that “star power” is the same as team building. It is not. Star power is the beginning. It is not the roster.

And that is the lesson the Knicks finally learned.

## Leon Rose stopped collecting names and started collecting fits

The Knicks finally changed when they stopped trying to win the press conference.

Leon Rose did not build this team like the old Knicks. The old Knicks would have chased the loudest name, overpaid the wrong star, and then spent the next three years pretending the pieces fit. Rose did something different. He built around Brunson, then kept asking the only question that actually matters:

Does this player make the team more complete in playoff basketball?

That is why the OG Anunoby trade mattered. Knicks fans loved RJ Barrett. Immanuel Quickley was good. Both were real players. But OG is the kind of player every playoff team needs and almost nobody wants to give up: size, defense, strength, enough shooting, no ego, no nonsense.

That is not as emotionally exciting as keeping the young guys and hoping they become perfect. But the point is not to collect players you like. The point is to build a team that survives.

Then came Mikal Bridges. Expensive? Yes. Five first-round picks is not light. But Bridges gave them another long wing, another defender, another player who can shoot, run, cut, and not break your lineup.

Then Karl-Anthony Towns gave them size and shooting at a level most centers cannot provide. Josh Hart gave them rebounding, toughness, and chaos. Mitchell Robinson gave them rim protection. Deuce McBride gave them guard defense and shooting. Brunson gave them the half-court engine.

That is a team.

Not just a collection of names. A team.

The Knicks finally built something where the pieces explain each other.

Brunson pressures the defense. Towns stretches size. OG and Bridges cover wings. Hart wins ugly possessions. Robinson cleans up mistakes. The roster has counters. It has armor. It has options.

That is what the Carmelo teams never had enough of.

## Playoff basketball punishes incomplete teams

This is the central point: playoff basketball is not regular season basketball.

In the regular season, you can hide a weak defender. You can survive a non-shooter. You can win because your star gets hot. You can beat tired teams. You can stack wins against bad teams. You can look better than you really are.

In the playoffs, every flaw becomes a target.

If your guard cannot defend, he is getting screened into the action.

If your wing cannot shoot, his defender is sitting in the paint.

If your center cannot move, he is defending in space.

If your star is the only creator, he is seeing traps, doubles, stunts, and bodies for seven games.

This is why “complete” matters more than “talented.”

The Nuggets at their peak were complete because Jokic solved the offense, Murray gave them shot-making, Gordon defended and cut, Porter spaced, and KCP handled dirty work.

OKC is complete because they have a star creator, waves of defenders, shooting, youth, and lineup flexibility.

Minnesota is complete because Edwards gives them pressure, while their size and defense make every possession uncomfortable.

And now the Knicks are complete because Brunson is no longer being asked to do everything alone. He is still the engine, but the roster has enough two-way players that the team does not collapse if the first option is taken away.

That is what the Knicks were chasing for 27 years without fully understanding it.

They kept looking for the answer.

The answer was the roster.

## So why did it take this long?

It took this long because the Knicks kept confusing activity with progress.

A trade is not progress if it makes the team older, thinner, and more expensive.

A star is not progress if the roster around him still makes no sense.

A playoff appearance is not progress if it convinces the front office to skip three steps.

A young player is not progress if he does not fit the championship version of the team.

The Knicks did not need to be perfect. They needed to be honest. For years, they were not honest about where they were. They tried to compete when they should have rebuilt. They tried to rebuild without fully committing. They tried to sell hope when the roster did not justify it.

Now, for the first time in a long time, the hope is not fake.

This Knicks team did not stumble into the Finals because New York wished hard enough. It got there because the front office finally understood what playoff teams actually need: creation, size, shooting, defense, rebounding, toughness, and enough roster balance that one injury, one bad matchup, or one cold shooting night does not destroy the entire identity.

That is why this run feels different.

It is not just that the Knicks are winning.

It is that the roster finally makes sense.

## The Knicks were not cursed. They were incomplete.

The Knicks’ 27-year wait was not just about bad luck. It was about bad timing, half-rebuilds, expensive shortcuts, old cores, incomplete rosters, and the refusal to choose a direction.

The Spurs got Duncan because one terrible year lined up with a generational prospect and a strong organization. The Knicks spent too much of their history trying to avoid that kind of bottom, but without building the kind of top that could actually win.

That is the lesson.

You can tank and still fail.

You can spend and still fail.

You can have a star and still fail.

But you cannot win deep in the playoffs without a complete team.

For 27 years, the Knicks were searching for the missing star, the missing trade, the missing quick fix. Leon Rose found the better answer: stop chasing the idea of a savior and build a roster that actually functions.

That is how you get out of purgatory.

That is how you get back to June.
