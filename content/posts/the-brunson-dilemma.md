# The Brunson Dilemma

Jalen Brunson did the thing every franchise is supposed to be looking for, and for the Knicks it almost felt impossible because of how long they had been looking for it. He gave them a direction.

That is really what separates a good player from a franchise player. It is not just the points. It is not just the fourth-quarter shot-making. It is not even just the playoff moments. It is that once you have him, the entire organization suddenly has a logic to it. The Knicks know what kind of team they are. They know what kind of roster they are trying to build. They know what the offense is supposed to look like late in games. They know who the ball belongs to when the season starts turning into possessions instead of quarters.

That alone is a miracle for a franchise that spent most of the last 25 years looking like it was building five different teams at once.

But that is also where the problem starts.

Because once you finally find the guy, you have to pay the guy. And once you pay the guy, the question becomes whether you are still building a basketball team or whether you are building a shrine around one player and calling that a roster.

That is the Brunson dilemma.

Not because Brunson is a problem. Brunson is the reason the Knicks are here. The problem is that winning creates bills. Every playoff run makes your players more expensive. Every role player who has a moment in May suddenly has a little more leverage in July. Every contender eventually has to decide which parts of the team were actually essential and which parts only looked essential because the system was working.

The Knicks finally became complete. Now they have to make sure they do not become top-heavy.

## Brunson’s contract is not the problem yet

The first thing that has to be said is that Brunson is not currently on the monster contract people assume he is on.

In 2024, Brunson signed a four-year, $156.5 million extension with the Knicks. The important part is not just the number. It is what he did not take. NBA.com reported that if Brunson waited, he could have signed a five-year deal worth $269 million, roughly $113 million more than the extension he took.[^1]

That matters because the story of Brunson’s contract is not that he has already squeezed the Knicks. It is actually the opposite. He gave the Knicks breathing room.

That breathing room helped the Knicks operate like a serious contender instead of a team trapped by its own star. It gave them a chance to keep building around him. It gave them flexibility in a league where the second apron is basically designed to punish teams that pay everyone as if money is fake.

But the future dilemma is still real.

Brunson turns 30 in 2026. Spotrac lists him at a $37.7 million cap hit for 2026-27, behind Karl-Anthony Towns at $57.1 million and OG Anunoby at $42.5 million.[^2] That is not some insane number for a star guard. In fact, relative to what he means to the Knicks, it is probably still a bargain.

The issue is what comes next.

At some point, Brunson is going to have another contract conversation. And when that conversation happens, the Knicks are not going to be paying for the 2024 discount. They are going to be paying for the face of the franchise, the playoff runs, the Garden moments, the culture shift, and the fact that without him none of this happens.

That is where emotions become expensive.

And this is exactly why front offices get themselves into trouble. They do not pay players only for future value. They pay them for memory. They pay them because the fan base would riot if they did not. They pay them because the name on the jersey has become bigger than the role inside the team.

That is how smart teams slowly become trapped teams.

## The Knicks are winning because they are not just Brunson

The best version of this Knicks team has not been the version where Brunson has to play superhero every night. It has been the version where Brunson is the engine, but not the entire car.

That sounds simple, but it is the exact difference between a dangerous playoff team and a team that can actually win four rounds.

The Knicks have had real depth. Not fake depth where a team has eight names people recognize but only five people the coach trusts. Real depth, where different players can swing a game without the team completely changing identity. The NBA’s playoff stats had Oklahoma City leading bench scoring, but the Knicks’ bench was elite in impact, ranking first in bench net rating at one point during the playoff run.[^3] That matters more than just counting points, because a bench is not only about scoring. It is about whether the team survives when the starters sit.

That is why a player like Landry Shamet becomes interesting. During the regular season, a minimum-type contract role player is just a role player. During the playoffs, if he has the right moments, he becomes proof of concept. He becomes someone fans want back. He becomes someone another team can talk itself into.

Spotrac lists Shamet on a one-year Knicks deal for 2025-26 worth about $3.08 million in salary, with a $2.3 million cap hit, and he is listed as a 2026 unrestricted free agent.[^4] That is exactly the kind of player who can become quietly expensive for a good team. Not max expensive. Not star expensive. But expensive enough that, when combined with three or four other decisions, the bench starts thinning out.

And the Knicks cannot afford to casually lose those players.

Not because Landry Shamet is some irreplaceable star. He is not. But because contenders die by subtraction. You lose one bench guard. Then you lose a backup big. Then your young player does not develop fast enough. Then one starter gets hurt. Then suddenly the difference between a Finals team and a second-round team is that the eighth man is not good enough anymore.

That is why depth is not a luxury for the Knicks. It is the thing keeping them from becoming every other top-heavy team.

## Mitchell Robinson is the real test

Mitchell Robinson is probably the cleanest example of the decision the Knicks are about to face.

He is not a normal player to value because he is not a normal box-score player. His impact is ugly. It is offensive rebounds. It is extra possessions. It is vertical pressure. It is making the other team feel like every shot at the rim has a body attached to it. It is also injuries, limited offense, and the constant question of how much money you can give to a center who may not always close every series.

Spotrac lists Robinson’s current deal as four years, $60 million, with a $12.95 million cap hit in 2025-26, and unrestricted free agency in 2026.[^5] That is a very manageable number for what he provides when healthy. But manageable numbers are usually not the numbers players sign after good playoff runs.

This is where the Knicks have to be honest.

Robinson is important. He is probably more important than casual fans understand. But the Knicks cannot treat every useful player as emotionally untouchable. That is how a roster becomes a museum. Everyone becomes part of the story, and no one can be moved.

The question is not “Do the Knicks like Mitchell Robinson?”

Of course they do.

The question is: at what number does keeping him preserve the roster, and at what number does keeping him begin to quietly break the roster?

That is the difference between a championship front office and a nostalgic one.

A championship front office loves its players, but it does not confuse love with pricing.

## The second apron is not a rule. It is a warning label.

The modern NBA is not just about having stars. It is about having stars at the right prices, role players who outperform their contracts, and enough draft picks or cheap rotation players to keep feeding the machine.

The 2025-26 NBA salary cap was set at $154.647 million. The first apron was $195.945 million and the second apron was $207.824 million.[^6] That second apron matters because it is not just a tax line. It limits how teams can operate. It makes trades harder. It restricts tools. It makes the cost of mistakes heavier.

This is where a lot of fans get the cap wrong. They think the owner just has to be willing to spend.

That used to be closer to true. It is not true anymore.

A rich owner can pay tax. A rich owner cannot ignore the second apron like it does not exist. The apron is not just about the check. It is about roster mobility. It is about whether you can aggregate salaries. It is about whether you can replace depth. It is about whether you can fix a mistake without creating three more.

That is why Brunson taking less mattered. It was not charity. It was team-building intelligence.

But the Knicks cannot waste that gift.

If the Knicks use Brunson’s discount just to become expensive anyway, then they lose the entire advantage he gave them. The point of Brunson taking less was not so everyone else could be overpaid. It was so the Knicks could keep an actual team around him.

## The Lakers are the cautionary tale

This is why the LeBron Lakers comparison matters.

LeBron James is one of the greatest players in the history of basketball. Maybe the greatest. But that is exactly why the Lakers are such a dangerous example. A name that big bends the room. The front office is not just making basketball decisions anymore. They are making legacy decisions, business decisions, ticket decisions, reputation decisions, and media decisions.

In 2025, LeBron exercised a $52.6 million player option with the Lakers for his 23rd NBA season.[^7] And the problem for the Lakers was not whether LeBron could still play. He obviously could. The problem was that the Lakers had to balance what LeBron still was with what the roster still needed.

That is the trap.

A player can still be great and still make team-building harder. A player can still deserve respect and still not be the cleanest fit for the next version of the team. A player can still sell jerseys and still force the front office into compromises that make the actual basketball worse.

The Knicks cannot let Brunson become that kind of figure prematurely.

He is not LeBron. He does not carry the same global brand weight. But in New York, with this fan base, after this playoff run, Brunson can become emotionally untouchable in a way that makes every future decision harder.

That does not mean trade him. That does not mean disrespect him. That does not mean nickel-and-dime the player who saved your franchise from being a punchline.

It means the Knicks have to separate gratitude from planning.

The player deserves gratitude.

The roster requires planning.

## The draft problem: how do you stay young when you traded the picks?

This is the next part of the dilemma.

The Knicks have already spent a lot of their future to build the present team. That is not a criticism. That is the price of contention. But it changes how they have to operate.

RealGM lists the Knicks with their own 2026 first-round pick, no 2027 first because it is owed to Brooklyn, a conditional 2028 first-round situation, no 2029 first because it is owed to Brooklyn, then their own first again in 2030.[^8] Bleacher Report summarized it similarly, noting that New York was left with only one first-rounder over the next two years but multiple second-rounders.[^9]

This means the Knicks cannot simply assume the next wave of talent will arrive naturally.

For a rebuilding team, mistakes are annoying. For a contender with limited picks, mistakes are expensive. You cannot miss on the margins. You cannot waste second-rounders. You cannot treat two-way contracts like filler. You cannot assume buyout veterans will solve everything. You need a development pipeline even while trying to win the Finals.

That is hard to do.

But it is also what serious teams do. Denver found ways to build around Jokic without turning the roster into a star museum. Oklahoma City built a machine of picks, young players, and contracts, though even they are now facing the bill of success. Reuters reported that OKC’s payroll was projected to jump significantly as Shai Gilgeous-Alexander, Jalen Williams, and Chet Holmgren moved into major deals.[^10]

That is the lesson. Even the teams that look perfectly built eventually have to pay everyone.

The difference is whether they planned for it before the bill arrived.

## The Jay Wright lesson

This is why the Jay Wright comparison is actually useful.

Jay Wright did not build Villanova around one player as if the whole program would collapse when that player left. He built a culture where the next guy understood the standard before he was asked to carry it. Brunson was special, obviously. Wright called him a rare “scorer and leader” when talking about his development.[^11] But the system was not just “give Jalen the ball and pray.”

It was habits. It was role clarity. It was toughness. It was players knowing what winning basketball looked like before they were stars.

That is what the Knicks have to steal from Villanova.

They cannot wait until Brunson declines to start thinking about life after Brunson. That is not disrespect. That is respect for what he helped build. The worst thing the Knicks could do is let Brunson become the entire structure, because then when he ages, the whole thing ages with him.

The better version is this: Brunson becomes the bridge.

He establishes the standard. He teaches the young players how to prepare. He makes New York a serious basketball place again. He gives the Knicks a real title window. And while that window is open, the front office quietly builds the next layer underneath him.

That is how you avoid falling off a cliff.

You do not replace Brunson when he is gone.

You build the organization so that no single player leaving destroys the identity.

## What the Knicks should do now

The Knicks should not panic. This is not a “trade everyone before they get expensive” argument. That is how teams outsmart themselves and ruin a good thing.

The Knicks should stay aggressive, but disciplined.

They should keep Brunson as the center of the team, but not make every future move a Brunson tribute. They should re-sign important role players, but only at numbers that preserve flexibility. They should treat Robinson as valuable, but not priceless. They should bring back Shamet if the number makes sense, but not chase a playoff memory into a bad contract. They should use every second-round pick like it matters, because for them it does. They should keep searching for cheap, playable wings and guards, because that is what every expensive team eventually runs out of.

Most importantly, they should understand what made this Knicks team different.

It was not just Brunson.

It was Brunson plus structure.

Brunson plus depth.

Brunson plus defense.

Brunson plus the right kind of role players.

Brunson plus a front office that finally understood basketball is not 2K, and you cannot just stack names and hope the chemistry arrives later.

The Knicks spent decades failing to find a player like Brunson. Now that they found him, the temptation will be to make every decision about keeping him happy and preserving the moment. That temptation is understandable. It is also dangerous.

The point is not to prepare for Brunson to fail.

The point is to prepare so Brunson does not have to carry failure.

That is the difference.

Bad teams ask the star to cover their mistakes.

Good teams use the star to multiply their strengths.

The Knicks finally have the star. Now they have to prove they are not just lucky enough to have him, but smart enough to build past him.
