# Knicks-Spurs Game 4 Report: The Largest Finals Comeback Ever

Game 4 should have buried the Knicks.

San Antonio had the lead, the size, the rhythm, and the kind of scoreboard cushion that usually turns the fourth quarter of an NBA Finals game into a formality. The Spurs led by 29, and every possession seemed to be pushing the series back toward balance.

Instead, the Knicks turned the night into history.

New York beat San Antonio 107-106, completed the largest comeback in NBA Finals history, and took a 3-1 series lead. It was not clean, and it was not logical for most of the night. But it was enough, because the Knicks kept finding one more possession after the game looked gone.

## What decided it

The comeback started with pressure.

The Knicks stopped letting San Antonio move through the game comfortably. Jalen Brunson kept forcing the defense to make decisions, Jose Alvarado changed the pace, and OG Anunoby kept giving New York the two-way force it needed while the Garden climbed back into the game.

The final possession turned the comeback from improbable to permanent. Brunson's miss stayed alive, Anunoby crashed into the play, and the tip-in with 1.2 seconds left gave the Knicks the lead for good.

That was the game: not one perfect possession, but a long series of refusals that ended with the ball on OG's fingertips.

## Why it mattered

The win changed the series math.

A loss would have made the Finals feel dangerous again. San Antonio would have tied the emotional weight of the series and forced the Knicks to keep chasing answers against Victor Wembanyama's length. Instead, New York left Game 4 one win from the title.

That matters because comebacks are not only about the final score. They change what both teams believe is possible. The Spurs had a game they should have closed. The Knicks now have proof that even a 29-point deficit in the Finals was not enough to break them.

## Postgame interview note

The postgame answers matched the shape of the comeback.

Anunoby stayed calm explaining the final putback, which fit the way he finished the night. Alvarado's presence mattered because his fourth-quarter minutes gave New York a pressure release when the offense needed another ball-handler and another burst of edge.

That is the clearest postgame summary of Game 4. The Knicks did not survive because one player turned the night into a solo act. They survived because Brunson kept pushing, Alvarado changed the tempo, and Anunoby finished the possession that turned the series.
