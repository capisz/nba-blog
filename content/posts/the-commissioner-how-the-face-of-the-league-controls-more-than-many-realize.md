# The Commissioner: How the Face of the League Controls More Than Many Realize

In my lifetime, the NBA has really had two commissioners: David Stern and Adam Silver. Both men ran the league differently. Both had different visions. Both had different goals. But the part fans sometimes forget is the most important part: the commissioner is not elected by the people, the players, or the cities. The commissioner works for the owners.

That is the job.

The commissioner’s responsibility is to protect the league, grow the product, expand revenue, and explain to the thirty ownership groups why the direction of the NBA will make them more money. That reality is why fans across every sport have always carried some distrust toward commissioners. Not because every outcome is fake. Not because every whistle is ordered from a league office. But because fans understand that there is a version of the league happening behind closed doors that they are not allowed to see.

And in the NBA, that distrust has only grown.

David Stern’s mission was obvious. He wanted the NBA to become an international empire built around stars, brands, rivalries, and dynasties. He understood that basketball was not just a game. It was theater. It was television. It was mythology.

Larry Bird and Magic Johnson were not just two players to Stern. They were characters in a larger story. Boston versus Los Angeles. East versus West. The farm kid from Indiana against the Hollywood point guard. The Celtics against the Lakers. Every time they stepped on the floor, the league had its cinematic battle.

Stern did not simply benefit from dynasties. He helped build an NBA that depended on them.

The Lakers, Celtics, Pistons, Bulls, Rockets, Spurs, Lakers again, and then the Heat. The league’s growth was carried by recognizable powers, recognizable villains, and recognizable faces. The NBA under Stern was not trying to pretend every team mattered equally. The league knew where the money was. It knew what teams pulled ratings. It knew which uniforms traveled.

Stern’s NBA was the age of the empire.

But eventually, that model started to crack. LeBron James arrived as the league’s next obvious face, but he was in Cleveland. That was not Los Angeles. That was not Boston. That was not Chicago. That was not New York. The league had its next generational player, but not in the kind of market that fit the old formula.

When LeBron left Cleveland for Miami, it felt like Stern’s final handoff. The old dynastic model was still alive, but the league was moving into something new. Stars were no longer waiting for the league to build the stage around them. They were building the stage themselves.

Then came Adam Silver.

Silver’s mission has also been clear from the beginning: make the NBA look more like the NFL.

In the NFL, there is almost no such thing as a “small market” in the way NBA fans talk about it. Green Bay can matter. Kansas City can matter. Buffalo can matter. Pittsburgh can matter. If the teams are good, people watch. The uniform, the city, and the market size become secondary to the product on the field.

That is the dream Silver has been chasing.

He does not want an NBA where the league has to pray for Lakers-Celtics, Knicks-Lakers, or Warriors-Cavs every year. He wants a league where Oklahoma City versus Indiana, Denver versus Miami, Milwaukee versus Phoenix, or San Antonio versus New York can be sold as the best basketball in the world simply because the teams earned their place.

In theory, that is good for the sport.

The problem is that when the commissioner openly wants parity, fans start watching every league-controlled mechanism with a different eye.

The draft lottery. The referee assignments. The whistle. The timing of foul trouble. The sudden rise of certain franchises. The convenient outcome. The unexplained no-call. The “basketball reason” that just happens to serve a larger league narrative.

This does not mean the league is scripted. But it does mean fans are not crazy for questioning incentives.

If the owners share in the league’s overall financial growth, then a result that hurts one team can still benefit the ownership class as a whole. A fan cares about his city, his roster, and his team’s pain. An owner cares about franchise value, media rights, expansion fees, sponsorships, and revenue sharing. Those are not always the same interests.

That is where the suspicion lives.

The commissioner is presented as the face of the league, but he is also the manager of ownership interests. He is the person who stands at the podium and explains the future. He tells the fans why the league is healthy. He tells the owners why the money is growing. He tells the media why the product is fair.

But fairness and profitability are not always the same thing.

Now add gambling.

This is where the modern NBA feels more compromised than ever. The league has embraced sportsbooks, betting content, odds integrations, and official gaming partnerships. The same league that asks fans to trust the integrity of every whistle is also financially tied to an ecosystem built around people wagering on those whistles, player props, injury reports, rotations, and outcomes.

That does not prove corruption. But it absolutely raises the stakes of perception.

A missed call is no longer just a missed call. A player sitting late is no longer just a coaching decision. A strange rotation is no longer just strategy. A referee pattern is no longer just incompetence. In the gambling era, every questionable moment feels like it carries money behind it.

That is dangerous for the league.

Sports are built on belief. Fans have to believe that what they are watching is real. They can accept bad calls. They can accept mistakes. They can accept incompetence. What they cannot accept is the feeling that the league’s financial incentives are quietly sitting in the background of every major outcome.

That is why the commissioner matters.

David Stern understood the value of dynasties. Adam Silver understands the value of parity. Both visions made business sense. Both visions helped the league grow. But both also remind fans that the NBA is not just a sport. It is a business run by billionaires, protected by a commissioner, and funded emotionally by cities that do not actually own the teams they love.

The owners own the franchises.

But the cities own the meaning.

That is why fans have to keep paying attention. Pay attention to the lottery. Pay attention to the whistle. Pay attention to the league’s public statements. Pay attention to the sportsbook partnerships. Pay attention to what the commissioner says and what the league quietly incentivizes.

Because the commissioner may work for the owners.

But the game still belongs to the people who care enough to watch.
